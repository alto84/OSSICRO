# QA Review — LEGAL reviewer — health-law / FDA regulatory counsel

**Model:** Fable 5 | claude-fable-5  
**Verdict:** The 312.52/312.3(b) black-letter core is sound and unusually well-disciplined, but the pages overstate what the TORO bounds (regulatory vs. tort exposure), leave the Tier 2/3 de facto-assumption line porous, oversell "structurally illegal," and contain one confirmed citation error (Form 1571 Field 14, not 16) that is disqualifying for a wiki whose first-order requirement is citation accuracy.

## Strengths
- The core syllogism on wiki/00-overview/legal-thesis-3123-vs-31252.md is correctly built on quoted regulatory text: 312.52(a) writing/enumeration/default rules and 312.52(b) transferee liability are accurately rendered, and the closing-sentence failure mode (a purported transfer to a non-entity leaves the obligation with the sponsor, exposing whoever tried to shed it) is exactly the right legal consequence to foreground.
- The confirmed-vs-interpretive discipline is genuinely rare and well executed: every page separates black-letter law from OSSICRO's analytical positions with explicit callouts, flags the 2015 S-I guidance and 2025 AI-credibility guidance as DRAFT, and the gating page's burden-shifting rule (ambiguity resolves toward gating; proposer must show a function appears on no matrix row) is how competent regulatory counsel would draft it.
- The TORO anatomy table (wiki/03-documents/transfer-of-regulatory-obligations-toro.md) is practitioner-grade — parties/term/enumerated schedule/retained-obligations recitation/oversight/hand-back mechanics — and correctly identifies that the 312.52(a) default rule makes the instrument's completeness load-bearing.
- The 19-row gating matrix (wiki/05-ossicro-system/non-delegable-functions-and-gates.md) is comprehensive and correctly sourced, including non-obvious rows counsel would want: G9's note that Part 54 self-disclosure survives in the S-I model, G13 FMV/AKS, G14 the manufacturer's supply decision as a counterparty judgment, and G19's stricter-than-law operating rule explicitly labeled as such.
- The Part 11 page gets the predicate-rule architecture right (Part 11 requires no record to exist; Parts 50/54/56/312 do) and correctly distinguishes the 2003 Scope-and-Application enforcement-discretion narrowing from repeal — a distinction most vendor literature botches.
- The investigator-obligations-never-transfer boundary (312.60-312.69 bind the individual personally; 312.52 reaches sponsor obligations only) is stated correctly and consistently across all six pages — this is the accountability floor and the wiki never wobbles on it.

## Findings
### [HIGH] wiki/03-documents/transfer-of-regulatory-obligations-toro.md
**Issue:** Confirmed factual error, repeated four times (lines 8, 18, 42, 72): the page states the CRO transfer-of-obligations disclosure is 'Field 16 of Form FDA 1571.' Per FDA's own 1571 instructions, Field 14 is 'CONTRACT RESEARCH ORGANIZATIONS (CROs)' — the yes/no questions plus the Continuation Page statement per 21 CFR 312.23(a)(1)(viii). Field 16 is the person responsible for safety review under 312.32 — a different, load-bearing concept. Line 72 even commits the engine to 'reconcile against the Form 1571 Field 16 listing,' so a validation rule built from this page would check the wrong field. For a project whose stated first-order requirement is regulatory accuracy, this is exactly the failure class the whole system claims to prevent. (Verified against FDA 1571 Form Instructions, FORM FDA 1571 SUPPLEMENT; the CFR citation 312.23(a)(1)(viii) itself is correct.)

### [HIGH] wiki/01-roles-responsibilities/micro-cro-accountable-layer.md
**Issue:** The page conflates regulatory enumeration with liability containment. Line 29: 'the TORO writing defines the micro-CRO's regulatory exposure precisely, obligation by obligation' — true only for FDA administrative exposure. Civil exposure does not track the TORO schedule: negligent undertaking (Restatement (2d) Torts §324A), subject-injury and wrongful-death claims, and contribution/indemnity claims from pharma counterparties attach to what the micro-CRO's people actually did, not to what the writing enumerated. A micro-CRO whose medical monitor misses a fatal safety signal is a defendant regardless of the schedule. The page contains no treatment of insurance (clinical-trial liability, E&O, medical-professional coverage for the employed medical monitor), indemnification structure, or subject-injury compensation — for the entity whose entire reason to exist is 'holding accountability,' the accountability analysis covers only one of the two sovereigns that can reach it.

### [HIGH] wiki/01-roles-responsibilities/micro-cro-accountable-layer.md
**Issue:** The Tier 2/3 boundary is presented as a bright line ('the tier boundary that matters legally is 2→3') but the page's own text makes it porous. Line 40 describes the micro-CRO as 'an employer/engager of the qualified humans who OWN gated judgments when the sponsor-investigator does not personally,' while the tier table classifies Tier 2 as 'Consulting; obligations still not transferred.' If the micro-CRO's medical monitor is the operative decision-maker on causality/expectedness at Tier 2 — with no TORO — that is assumption-in-fact of the sponsor's 312.32 obligation: 312.52(b) attaches to a CRO 'that assumes any obligation,' BIMO looks at conduct rather than paper, and simultaneously the arrangement flunks the 312.52(a) writing requirement and leaves the S-I holding an obligation someone else actually discharged. The construct as drafted invites the worst posture available: CRO enforcement exposure without the TORO's definitional protection.

### [MEDIUM] wiki/00-overview/legal-thesis-3123-vs-31252.md
**Issue:** The thesis overstates its legal conclusion. Line 47 calls pure software disintermediation 'not merely a weak business model but a structurally unlawful one' (echoed at cro.md line 36, 'structurally foreclosed by regulation,' and in docs/detailed-plan.md, 'structurally illegal'). What 312.52 forecloses is software as TRANSFEREE. It is entirely lawful for a sponsor to use software to discharge coordination labor while retaining every obligation — that is the existing eClinical vendor industry. Compounding this, the Science 37 / TrialSpark collapses are offered as 'the market corroborat[ing] the law,' but neither company ever attempted a §312.52 transfer to software; they failed commercially as coordination vendors, which is evidence about margins, not legality. A regulator or opposing counsel reading 'structurally illegal' next to that evidence will conclude the author conflated two different propositions — and the credibility cost lands on the parts of the thesis that are actually right.

**Recommendation:** {f.get('recommendation','')}

### [MEDIUM] wiki/05-ossicro-system/part-11-and-ai-credibility.md
**Issue:** The clause-by-clause table asserts 'OSSICRO implementation' of §11.10 controls, but Part 11 compliance is a property of a deployed system plus the regulated user's procedures, never of distributed code — FDA's consistent position is that no product is 'Part 11 compliant' out of the box. The page omits every user-side obligation: 11.10(a) validation must be performed on the regulated user's installed instance (not just the project's 'versioned validation suite'); §11.100(b) requires the ORGANIZATION to verify individual identity before assigning an e-signature; and §11.100(c) requires the user organization — here, a solo sponsor-investigator — to submit a paper certification with a handwritten signature to FDA before its e-signatures are legally binding. The page cites 11.100(c) in passing but never tells the S-I this letter is their personal filing obligation. For an audience of physicians with no research office, silence on user-side duties converts 'Part 11-compliant substrate' (also asserted in docs/detailed-plan.md §2) into precisely the vendor overclaim the HARD PRINCIPLE forbids.

### [MEDIUM] wiki/03-documents/transfer-of-regulatory-obligations-toro.md
**Issue:** Two related overstatements about what transfer accomplishes. (1) Line 38: 'The 312.52 transfer moves direct enforcement exposure for the enumerated items' reads as a liability shield; FDA's actual practice (BIMO CPGM 7348.810, warning letters) is that the sponsor remains citable for failures in transferred functions via the retained oversight duty, and FDA has never treated 312.52 as immunizing the transferor. The page's own E6(R3) hedge two sentences later contradicts the headline. (2) The model enumeration's 'Split' rows (312.32 'preparation and submission mechanics' transferred, judgment retained; 312.33 drafting transferred, ownership retained) have no textual basis in 312.52, which speaks of transferring 'obligations set forth in this part' — sub-obligation splitting is common industry practice, but FDA could treat the entity that prepares and submits a 15-day report as having assumed 312.32 wholesale. The interpretive flag exists but understates the contestability of the split.

### [MEDIUM] wiki/05-ossicro-system/non-delegable-functions-and-gates.md
**Issue:** Gate property 1 ('There is no configuration flag, permission mode, or API call that executes the gated act') is a code-integrity representation the project cannot keep under its own MIT license: anyone may fork, strip the gates, and redistribute. The legal architecture leans on 'enforced in code' (PROGRAM.md hard principle) without addressing distribution integrity — which creates (a) misrepresentation exposure if 'OSSICRO' builds circulate with gates removed, (b) foreseeable-misuse arguments in any subject-injury suit, and (c) no stated mechanism (trademark policy, signed builds, gate self-test in the validation suite) to scope the compliance claims to official artifacts. This also interacts with the Part 11 finding: 11.10(a) requires the ability to discern altered records/systems, and a gate-stripped fork is exactly the alteration the compliance story must be able to detect or disclaim.

### [MEDIUM] wiki/00-overview/legal-thesis-3123-vs-31252.md
**Issue:** The Mode B + micro-CRO structure has an unaddressed structural conflict: in a sponsor-investigator IND, monitoring under 312.56 exists to verify the conduct of the investigator — the same individual who, wearing the sponsor hat, selects, hires, pays, and can fire the micro-CRO supplying the monitors. FDA's 2015 S-I draft guidance (cited on this very page) and BIMO inspection experience treat the absence of an independent sponsor watching the investigator as the S-I model's characteristic weakness; the 1571 instructions reinforce it by stating that for S-I INDs the investigator personally holds the field 15/16 monitoring and safety-review responsibilities. Neither this page, micro-cro-accountable-layer.md, nor the TORO model enumeration (which transfers 312.53(d)/312.56(a) to the micro-CRO) says a word about monitor independence, escalation duties that survive the client relationship, or what the micro-CRO's monitor does upon finding serious noncompliance by the person paying the invoice.

### [MEDIUM] wiki/00-overview/legal-thesis-3123-vs-31252.md
**Issue:** Step 2 of the 'three confirmed steps' (line 44) misreads a canon: 21 U.S.C. 321(e) says 'person' INCLUDES individual, partnership, corporation, and association — an illustrative list, not a closed one, under both the standard 'includes' canon and FDCA practice. 'Software is not an individual, partnership, corporation, or association' therefore does not complete the syllogism as a matter of statutory text. The conclusion is still right, but its sound basis is step 3 (legal personality / enforceability) plus 312.3(b)'s 'independent contractor' language — a role only a juridical person can occupy — not enumeration-exclusion. A page that labels its steps 'confirmed' should not have a canon error in one of them.
