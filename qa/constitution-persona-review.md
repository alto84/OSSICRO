# OSSICRO Constitution — Key-User Persona Review (Fable, 2026-07-09)


## Patient / research participant
**Verdict:** Protects me in structure and in spirit — the hard constraints around consent, exculpatory language, and safety clocks are real and I benefit from every one of them — but the Constitution speaks about me, never to me or for me. I am the mission's justification and CP1's missing reader. My strongest concrete protections (privacy boundary, therapeutic-misconception guardrails, injury-compensation honesty, portal transparency) live one tier below the document that outranks them, and the one thing guaranteed by hard constraint is that I will never see the machine's fingerprints on the form I sign. Good faith, incomplete promise: I would sign up under this system, but I would want the five additions above in writing first.

I read this document looking for myself, and here is the honest answer: it protects me sincerely, but it barely addresses me. The Constitution's first value is my safety, and it means it — HC1 keeps the consent conversation a human act, HC6 makes it structurally impossible for a form to trick me into waiving my rights, HC3 means the safety-report deadlines that exist for my body are computed and never fudged, and CP3 contains the one sentence in the whole document that actually faces me: consent documents speak plainly, with risks undiminished and payment never framed as benefit. I believe the people who wrote this were thinking about me. But everywhere else, I am the object of protection, never the addressee. CP1 measures a document's adequacy against 'the reader's decision' and then lists the readers: regulator, IRB member, medical monitor, manufacturer. I am not on the list — and I am the reader of the most consequential document this system produces, deciding whether to put an experimental compound in my body. Meanwhile, the things that protect me most concretely — the privacy state machine that keeps my record from leaving my doctor's system while I'm just looking, the therapeutic-misconception guardrails, the blocking rule that forces an honest pre-consent answer about injury compensation, the portal transparency floor — all live in the wiki persona page and subordinate artifacts, one tier below a Constitution that explicitly says it wins when they conflict. The persona page is excellent; it confronts therapeutic misconception and the dual-role hazard head-on, in language that respects me. But a wiki page is a description, not a promise. And there is one thing the Constitution actively guarantees I will never know: HC7 scrubs every trace of AI drafting from the documents I sign. The system logs AI involvement meticulously for regulators (HC5) and hides it completely from me. Maybe that's defensible — ghostwriting is normal — but a document this careful about honesty should defend that choice in writing rather than not notice it made one.

### Gaps
- **[high]** The patient is not a named reader in CP1's adequacy test. Adequacy is measured against 'the reader's decision' for regulators, IRB members, medical monitors, and manufacturers — but the informed-consent form's reader is the subject, deciding whether to participate.  
  _why:_ The ICF is the one document whose failure lands on my body, not on a filing. If adequacy-against-the-reader's-decision is the system's quality anchor, my decision — can I actually understand, from this document, what I'm agreeing to and that research is not treatment — must be an anchored case, not an inference from CP3's register rule.
- **[high]** Patient privacy has no hard constraint. The looking-vs-enrolling boundary (no PHI leaves the covered entity before authorization) is code-enforced per the wiki's privacy-state-machine, but appears nowhere in Section III.  
  _why:_ It plainly passes the earned-rule test the Constitution itself defines: a privacy breach is irreversible (condition 1), whether PHI crossed a boundary is a fact (condition 2), and flexibility is exploitable (condition 3). A protection that is already enforced in code but absent from the constraint list can be deprioritized or refactored away without ever triggering the amendment process, because the Constitution outranks the artifact that holds it.
- **[medium]** HC7 guarantees I can never tell from my consent form that AI drafted it, and no artifact anywhere promises me disclosure of AI involvement — the choice is not even acknowledged as a choice.  
  _why:_ The system tells regulators everything about AI involvement (HC5, Part 11) and tells me nothing, by hard constraint. The signer-adopts-it theory (CP2) is a real defense — physicians sign ghostwritten documents routinely — but a constitution whose second value is honesty, and whose consent doctrine turns on my understanding, should state and justify the non-disclosure position rather than leave it as a silent consequence of an anti-leakage rule aimed at process contamination.
- **[medium]** The therapeutic-misconception and dual-role protections — mandatory research-vs-treatment language, independent consent discussant, the triage question of whether expanded access is the honest frame — exist only in the wiki, not the Constitution.  
  _why:_ The mission statement itself carries the hazard ('patients' access to early-phase therapies') and the persona page admits the entry framing seeds therapeutic misconception. The mitigations are thoughtful but constitutionally unprotected: under speed pressure (value 3 is explicitly a patient-access value), nothing in Sections II–IV stops a future revision from trimming the independent-discussant default or the portal counterweights, because none of them are anchored above the artifact level.
- **[low]** The amendment process (Section V) has no patient-harm input. Revision is driven by regulation changes, capability changes, and 'documents that fail downstream' — with no channel through which a subject's experience of harm, confusion, or coercion feeds back.  
  _why:_ Every named feedback loop terminates at a professional reader. If a patient signed a consent form they did not actually understand, that failure is invisible to the hindsight loop unless a regulator or IRB happens to catch it. The system that exists for my protection has no ear turned toward me.

### Suggested additions
- Add the subject as a named reader in CP1: 'A consent document is adequate when the prospective subject — at the comprehension level of the enrolling population — could decide, correctly and freely, from this document.' This makes patient comprehension a first-class adequacy failure, not a register nuance under CP3.
- Add a hard constraint HC8 (privacy boundary): no PHI leaves the covered-entity boundary before a signed authorization or IRB waiver; matching operates only on local, de-identified data. Justify it under the earned-rule test the Constitution already provides — it passes all three conditions and the enforcement code already exists.
- Anchor the research-is-not-treatment guarantee at Constitution level: every patient-facing document and portal surface in a research pathway states that the study is research, and no status language implies availability or likelihood of benefit before IRB approval and clinical judgment. One sentence in Section IV would protect everything the persona page builds.
- State a position on AI-drafting disclosure to subjects: either commit to a disclosure line in patient-facing materials, or write the reasoned justification for the signer-adopts-it model in the Constitution so the non-disclosure is a defended decision rather than an accident of HC7.
- Add a patient-feedback clock to Section V: subject-originated events — comprehension failures surfaced at consent, withdrawal-experience reports, complaints to the IRB — feed rubric revision alongside regulatory and capability changes.
- Make the injury-compensation honesty rule (the Mode B blocking completeness-ledger item: the ICF must state plainly whether any compensation or coverage exists, determined before consent) traceable to a constitutional sentence, since it is the single disclosure most likely to be uncomfortable for the physician-operator and therefore most in need of protection from above.

## Enrolling physician
**Verdict:** Serves me with reservations. The scope clarity (HC1's closed list), the anti-fabrication and provenance spine, and the honest residual-burden accounting are exactly what would let a practicing physician trust this system with career-level stakes. But the Constitution is written entirely in the direction of what I owe it and my patients; it constitutionalizes none of what the system owes me — defect disclosure, tractable review, competence flagging, change notification. Fix the asymmetry and I sign. As written, I'd use it — warily, and with my own counsel on speed-dial.

Mostly, yes — and where it serves me, it serves me well. HC1 is the sentence I needed before I'd touch this: a closed, enumerated list of what the software will never do, with consent, causality, sign-offs, signatures, and submission release named explicitly and enforced in code that fails closed. I don't have to trust marketing; I can read the list. CP2 ("write as the signer") gets the accountability physics right — this is my professional work product, and the system holds itself to my standard, not to 'AI content for review.' HC2/HC4/HC5 are inspection armor: every span traceable, every citation resolving, every AI contribution logged with my disposition attached. When BIMO shows up, that provenance record is the difference between 'defensible' and 'career-ending.' HC3 computing my 7/15-day clocks while explicitly leaving the causality trigger to me is exactly the division of labor I want. And Section II saying speed is a patient-access value — that delay IS the failure mode — tells me the authors understand why I'm here: my patient doesn't have time for a system that hedges itself into paralysis. The companion persona page earns real trust by under-promising: 80–150 setup hours in Mode B, 10–20 h/month standing, and the flat 'OSSICRO cannot solve insurance' admission. But the Constitution is one-directional. It is meticulous about what I owe — every signature, every judgment, every accountability — and nearly silent about what the system owes ME when it fails. 'Signed by an accountable human' is the theory of legitimacy; I am that human, and the document that makes me the backstop for every error says nothing about defect disclosure to me, nothing about what happens when a gate-passing draft turns out wrong after I've signed it, nothing about whether I'll be told when a regulation change invalidates something already in my IND. CP2's standard — would I adopt this sentence 'reading closely' — quietly assumes I will closely read a several-hundred-page IND package. Either that assumption is real and my time is not respected, or it isn't and I'm a rubber stamp with a license. The single-pass-review triage that resolves this lives in a wiki page, not in the Constitution that outranks it. And CP6 routes every reserved decision to 'the accountable human' without ever asking whether I'm competent to make it — I'm a physician, not a statistician or a CMC chemist, and the document never obligates the system to tell me when I'm the wrong reviewer.

### Gaps
- **[high]** No reciprocal obligation to the signer: the Constitution never states what OSSICRO owes the physician when a gate-passing draft is later found defective — no duty of defect disclosure, no error notification to affected signers, no support commitment at inspection. Section V routes downstream failures into 'rubric revision' (system self-improvement), not into telling the human who signed the flawed document.  
  _why:_ I am the entire accountability layer. A constitution that makes me the backstop for every error while owing me nothing when the error originates in the machine is asymmetric in exactly the way that would make a careful physician walk away. 21 CFR 312.68 inspection exposure is mine alone; the least the system can constitutionalize is 'if we learn our draft was wrong, you hear it first.'
- **[high]** Review tractability and automation bias are unaddressed at the constitutional level. CP2's standard is adoption 'reading closely,' but nothing in Sections II–IV governs how the system keeps close reading humanly possible, or guards against the known failure where a polished, complete-looking draft induces LESS scrutiny than a rough one. The single-pass-review UX and inferred-vs-deterministic span segregation exist only in subordinate wiki artifacts.  
  _why:_ This is the primary user's primary failure mode. If the review burden is untriaged, my only options are heroic hours (defeats the mission) or rubber-stamping (defeats the legitimacy theory). The thing that makes my signature real — attention triaged to the judgment spans — deserves a principle in the document that wins conflicts, not a page that loses them.
- **[medium]** Reviewer competence is assumed, never verified or flagged. CP6 sends every reserved decision to 'the accountable human'; HC1 says OSSICRO never gives statistical sign-off — but for a solo sponsor-investigator there is no statistician unless someone tells me I need one. The Constitution never obligates the system to surface 'this decision requires competence you may not hold' (stats, CMC, pharm/tox). Micro-CRO escalation exists only in the persona page.  
  _why:_ Routing a decision to an unqualified human is not meaningfully safer than automating it — it just relocates the failure to the person holding the license. A physician 'qualified by training and experience' per 312.53(a) for the drug is not thereby qualified for every reserved judgment in the matrix.
- **[medium]** No user-facing notification duty on regulatory change. Section V versions rules against amended citations and keeps a regulatory-change log, but says nothing about telling a physician mid-study that an amendment touches documents they already signed or SOPs they operate under.  
  _why:_ My IND is perpetual maintenance (312.33, 312.32). A change log that updates the engine but not the human running a live study protects future drafts, not the current investigator — and I'm the one FDA holds to the current regulation.
- **[low]** No constitutional duty to say 'don't proceed.' The mission is to make the sponsor-investigator role tractable, and Value 3 rewards delivered documents; the persona page's triage hard-stops (insurance, billing, site infeasibility) show the right instinct, but the Constitution itself never states that the most useful output can be a well-reasoned no before drafting begins.  
  _why:_ A tool constitutionally oriented toward producing packages has a structural lean toward keeping me moving. The persona page's 'a physician who discovers this gap after assembling the package has been failed by the tool' is exactly the right principle — it belongs in the document that outranks everything else.
- **[low]** The Part 11 attribution log (HC5) records my identity and disposition on every AI contribution but the Constitution doesn't address what that record means for me — it is discoverable evidence of exactly how I reviewed, cutting both ways at inspection or in litigation.  
  _why:_ I'd rather know upfront that the audit trail documenting my diligence also documents my haste. Honest framing of the double edge would increase trust, not reduce it.

### Suggested additions
- A 'Duties to the signer' section (or CP8): if OSSICRO learns that a delivered draft contained a material defect — factual, citational, or regulatory — every affected signer is notified with the specific spans, before any rubric-revision bookkeeping. The system's first obligation on discovering its own error runs to the human who signed it.
- Elevate review-tractability to a principle: the system must keep 'reading closely' humanly achievable — judgment-bearing and inferred spans triaged to the front, deterministic/boilerplate spans segregated and individually verifiable — and must treat a review interface that invites rubber-stamping as a safety defect, not a UX preference.
- A competence-flagging clause under CP6: when a reserved decision requires professional competence outside what the accountable human is known to hold (statistics, CMC, pharm/tox), the system says so explicitly and names the escalation path (consultant, Micro-CRO/TORO) rather than silently routing the call to whoever is available.
- A regulatory-change notification duty in Section V: when an amended authority touches a document a physician has already signed or a live obligation they carry, the change is surfaced to that physician with the affected artifacts identified — the change log is not complete until the affected humans know.
- State the triage-refusal principle constitutionally: OSSICRO's usefulness value includes telling the physician, before drafting effort or fees are spent, that a route is infeasible (insurance gap, billing non-qualification, site incapability) — a package that should not have been assembled is a failure even if every document in it is perfect.

## Micro-CRO (the accountable entity)
**Verdict:** Defensible foundation, incomplete counterparty. The Constitution enforces the Micro-CRO's legal thesis (312.52: only an inspectable entity holds sponsor obligations) better than any contract could — HC1's closed fail-closed gates, computed clocks, and the provenance/Part 11 substrate are genuine liability armor. But it governs a software-and-physician dyad and leaves the accountable entity unnamed: no TORO-scope binding, no whose-human resolution at Tier 3, no reciprocal validation/incident/retention obligations to the party that carries enforcement parity. Adopt for Tiers 0-2 as written; do not operate Tier 3 under it until the entity-facing amendments land.

I am the entity that gets inspected, warned, and enjoined. When BIMO shows up, it is my records, my SOPs, my staff qualifications, and my name on the 483. Read from that chair, this Constitution is the best liability armor a technology vendor has ever handed me — and it does not know I exist.

What genuinely serves me: HC1 is my legal thesis stated as an engineering constraint. The closed, enumerated, fail-closed gate list means I can tell an inspector, in writing, that no reserved act can be performed by the software layer under any configuration — no flag, no API path. That is the difference between 'we have a policy' and 'the violation is structurally impossible,' and it is exactly what my 312.52(b) exposure requires, because when I assume an obligation whose discharge requires judgment, the regulation demands I staff that judgment with a qualified human, and HC1 plus CP5 ('judgment escalates; it never clears') and CP6 (doubt routes to the human) guarantee the software cannot quietly become that human. HC3 removes my single most common enforcement exposure — the blown 15-day safety clock — from the judgment layer entirely; a computed deadline is a deadline I can defend. HC4 and HC5 together are my inspection-readiness substrate: when the investigator asks 'who produced this span and on what basis,' provenance-plus-Part-11-attribution means I answer from the record, not from memory. HC2's traceability requirement means a fabricated citation is a detected event, not a latent poison in a filing that carries my enforcement parity. And the amendment section's regulatory-change log — flagging every rule a CFR amendment touches — is a real gift to my SOP change-control burden.

What does not serve me: the Constitution is written for a two-party world — the software and the physician-signer — and I am the third party that makes Tier 3 legal. 'The accountable human' appears throughout, and CP2 defines the signer as 'a licensed physician,' but when a TORO transfers safety-report submission operations to me, the accountable human for that obligation is MY qualified staff member, who may be a CRA or a safety officer, not a physician, and not the sponsor-investigator. The governing document's ambiguity about whose human holds which gate is, for me, liability ambiguity. Worse: my single most important legal protection under 312.52 — silence means retained; a partial transfer must enumerate each obligation — has no counterpart in the Constitution. Nothing binds the system's behavior to the TORO's enumerated scope, so nothing stops the pipeline from cheerfully performing operations I never assumed, creating implied-assumption optics I would have to litigate away. And the Constitution is silent on the obligations the software owes ME as its regulated user: I must validate it as a computerized system under Part 11 (HC5 is attribution, not CSV), I must maintain E6(R3) oversight evidence over it as a service-provider technology, I must retain its records for 312.57(c) periods, and I must feed its failures into my deviation/CAPA system — CP7's hindsight loop improves rubrics, but it never promises to tell me when a gate failed or a fabrication slipped through, and an incident I don't hear about is a deviation I can't report.

Net: this document protects my legal thesis better than any contract I could draft, and it protects the patient and the physician with real teeth. But it protects me by implication only. The hard line it vows to defend — reserved acts stay with accountable humans — is my line; I need the document to say which humans, under which written instrument, with what support obligations flowing back to the entity that carries the enforcement parity.

### Gaps
- **[high]** The Micro-CRO entity is never named in the Constitution; 'the accountable human' is systematically conflated with the physician-signer (CP2: 'a licensed physician signs each document')  
  _why:_ At Tier 3, the accountable human for a TORO-assumed obligation is Micro-CRO staff (a qualified monitor, a safety officer, a medical monitor the entity engages) — not the sponsor-investigator. When the governing document doesn't distinguish whose human discharges which gate, the entity cannot map the Constitution's human-gate guarantees onto its 312.52(b) enforcement exposure, and an inspector asking 'who was the accountable reviewer for this assumed obligation' finds a document that assumes the answer is always the physician.
- **[high]** No TORO-scope binding: nothing in the Constitution constrains system behavior to the obligations actually enumerated in the written transfer  
  _why:_ 312.52(a)'s 'silence means retained' is the entity's core protection — obligations not described in writing stay with the sponsor. A pipeline that performs operations outside the TORO's enumerated scope creates implied-assumption optics and drags the entity toward accountability it never contracted for. The gating matrix gates non-delegable acts; nothing gates delegable-but-not-delegated ones.
- **[high]** No obligations flow from the software to its regulated user: no commitment to computerized-system validatability, versioned/change-controlled releases, or vendor-oversight support artifacts  
  _why:_ The Micro-CRO must validate OSSICRO under Part 11 (CSV, e-signature control) and maintain documented E6(R3) service-provider oversight over it. HC5 covers attribution of AI contributions, not system validation. A governing document that binds the software's outputs but promises nothing about the software's own auditability as a regulated system leaves the entity's vendor-oversight SOP with nothing contractual to stand on.
- **[medium]** No incident-disclosure or deviation/CAPA hook: the system is not obligated to surface its own failures (gate violations, fabrications caught late, provenance breaks) to the accountable entity  
  _why:_ The entity's mandatory SOP set includes deviation/CAPA. CP7's hindsight loop feeds rubric revision — a quality-improvement channel, not a compliance channel. A GateViolation that fails closed silently is safe for the patient but leaves the entity unable to document, investigate, and CAPA an event it is regulatorily required to know about.
- **[medium]** No record-retention or export commitment matching 312.57(c) and BIMO inspection needs  
  _why:_ HC4/HC5 create excellent records but the Constitution never commits to their durability, retention period (2 years post-approval/discontinuation), or inspection-time exportability. The retention obligation is the entity's; if the audit trail lives only inside the vendor's system with no guaranteed export, the entity holds an obligation it cannot independently discharge.
- **[low]** CP1's enumerated readers (regulator, IRB member, medical monitor, manufacturer) omit the Micro-CRO itself as a decision-making reader  
  _why:_ The entity makes real decisions from OSSICRO outputs — TORO intake scoping, monitoring-plan adequacy, inspection responses. CP1's adequacy-against-the-reader's-decision test should name the entity's decisions among those the documents must serve; today the entity is invisible even in the principle designed to enumerate who documents are for.

### Suggested additions
- Name the entity: add a section (or extend CP2/CP6) distinguishing the three accountability holders — sponsor-investigator, Micro-CRO staff under a TORO, external parties (IRB/DSMB/QP) — and state which gates route to which holder's qualified human. 'The accountable human' should always resolve to a role with a legal basis.
- Add a TORO-scope constraint (candidate HC or strong CP): when operating under a Tier-3 engagement, the system performs only operations traceable to an obligation enumerated in the written transfer; anything outside scope is flagged to the sponsor-investigator as retained. Justify via the exploitability prong — scope creep is exactly the 'just this once' flexibility HC1 already refuses.
- Add reciprocal obligations to the regulated user: versioned, change-controlled releases; validation documentation sufficient for the entity's Part 11 CSV; a defined oversight interface (periodic audit-trail summaries, gate-violation and fabrication-catch incident feed) that the entity can file as E6(R3) oversight evidence and route into its deviation/CAPA SOP.
- Add a records covenant: audit trail, provenance, and attribution records are retained and exportable in inspection-ready form for at least the 312.57(c) period, independent of the entity's continued commercial relationship with the software.
- Extend CP1's reader enumeration to include the Micro-CRO's own decisions (TORO scoping, oversight review, inspection response) as decisions generated documents must adequately serve.
- Amend CP2 to cover non-physician signers: monitor visit reports, safety-processing dispositions, and entity SOP-governed work product are signed by qualified Micro-CRO staff; 'write as the signer' should name the signer role per document category, not default to the physician.

And the persona page is honest where it counts: 'OSSICRO produces the package that lets pharma say yes; it cannot make pharma say yes.' Correct — my supply decision is non-delegable and they wrote that down. So: does the Constitution serve me? Genuinely, yes, as far as it reaches. ### Suggested additions

## The Regulator
**Verdict:** Conditionally favorable. The legal thesis is correct, the gating matrix names the right non-delegables with the right authorities, and this is more regulator-literate than the median sponsor-investigator operation I inspect. But the Constitution proves the human was present, not that the human reviewed; it describes gate enforcement without committing to validation evidence; and it scrubs AI traces without a disclosure posture. Speaking as the person who receives these packages: the legal spine is genuinely sound, and I want to say that first because most AI-in-clinical-research pitches get it wrong. The gating matrix names the right non-delegables with the right citations (consent as an event not a document, the IRB's 56.111 judgment, causality under 312.32, the 1571/1572 signatures as personal legal commitments), and the confirmed-vs-interpretive labeling is something I wish every sponsor did. HC3 is welcome — I have seen far more missed 15-day reports from arithmetic and calendar confusion than from bad faith. And the document names the Mode B self-monitoring conflict instead of hoping I would not notice; that is exactly the weakness BIMO flags in sponsor-investigator studies, and naming it with contractual mitigations earns real credibility. But here is my problem, and it is the central one: the entire theory of legitimacy is 'signed by an accountable human,' and nothing in this Constitution gives me evidence that the signature is a review rather than a click. HC5 logs the reviewer's identity, timestamp, and disposition — that tells me a human was present, not that a human engaged. Automation bias is the documented failure mode of exactly this architecture, and a system that makes drafting nearly free will produce document volume that a solo practicing clinician cannot substantively review. When document polish stops correlating with sponsor competence — and this system's whole purpose is to break that correlation — I lose my oldest heuristic for triaging risk, and the Constitution offers me nothing to replace it. Net: this is the most regulator-literate AI governance document I have been shown, and I would rather receive an OSSICRO package than the median S-I submission. ### Gaps
- **[high]** Review substance is unverified — the signature is the entire legitimacy theory but nothing measures or enforces genuine reviewer engagement (rubber-stamp / automation-bias risk)  
  _why:_ HC5 records identity, timestamp, and disposition — presence, not engagement. A system that makes drafting nearly free generates volume a solo clinician cannot substantively review, and the Constitution contains no reviewer-workload limits, engagement evidence (time-on-document, annotation requirements, forced interaction with flagged spans), or spot-audit mechanism. From the regulator's chair, 'a human signed it' without evidence of review is precisely the failure mode I inspect for.
- **[high]** Gate enforcement is asserted, not evidenced — no software validation commitment for the gate mechanism itself  
  _why:_ 'Enforced in code... no configuration flag or API path around it' is architecture prose. 21 CFR 11.10(a) requires systems validation, and FDA's AI-credibility direction (draft 2025 guidance, which the wiki itself cites) will demand documented validation, change control on engine/registry/gates.json, and adversarial test evidence. The Constitution should obligate a validation artifact, not just describe the design.
- **[high]** Safety-clock trigger is gameable — HC3 computes from the human causality determination but does not guard determination latency  
  _why:_ The known 312.32(c) evasion is not miscomputing the 15 days; it is sitting on the qualifying determination that starts them. HC1 reserves the determination to a human and HC3 computes flawlessly after it, but nothing tracks initial-receipt date or escalates a stalled determination. The hard constraint protects the step that was never the problem.
- **[medium]** No affirmative AI-use disclosure posture toward FDA or the IRB  
  _why:_ HC7 removes every trace of the drafting process from output while HC5 keeps the attribution record internal. The net effect is that the receiving regulator learns of AI involvement only on inspection. The style-contamination rationale for HC7 is legitimate, but unpaired with a disclosure commitment it reads as concealment — and transparency is the direction of travel in FDA's AI guidance.
- **[medium]** G4 medical monitor independence is unspecified for the solo sponsor-investigator case  
  _why:_ The matrix routes causality/expectedness to a 'medical monitor / qualified safety physician' but never requires that person be independent of the S-I whose trial it is. The document handles monitor independence carefully for 312.56 monitoring (TORO covenants) but is silent on safety-judgment independence — as an IRB member reviewing a Mode B protocol, I would condition approval on knowing who G4's human is.
- **[medium]** The system destroys the regulator's oldest triage signal — document quality as a proxy for sponsor operational competence — and offers nothing to replace it  
  _why:_ OSSICRO's mission is to give physicians with no research office submissions indistinguishable in polish from experienced sponsors'. That is the point, but it means I can no longer infer readiness from the package. A verifiable operational-readiness attestation (the wiki's site-qualification dossier gestures at this) belongs in the constitutional frame, not just the product periphery.
- **[low]** Fate of 'inferred' flags under HC2 vs HC7 is ambiguous  
  _why:_ HC2 permits unsourced content if marked inferred and flagged for the reviewer; HC7 strips process traces from output. The Constitution does not state whether an inferred flag the signer never explicitly resolves can survive into, or be silently laundered out of, a submitted document. A one-sentence rule — no document releases with an unresolved inferred flag — would close it.

### Suggested additions
- A review-substance requirement in Section III or IV: reviewer workload ceilings, mandatory interaction with every flagged/inferred span before signature, and periodic spot-audits of review engagement — evidence that signatures are reviews, recorded in the same Part 11 trail
- A gate-validation obligation: the gate mechanism (gates.json and its enforcement path) carries a documented validation package with adversarial test evidence and change control, versioned like the regulatory-logic artifacts it protects
- Extend HC3 to guard the trigger, not just the arithmetic: track initial-receipt date for every safety event and escalate to the human (and to the micro-CRO's independent path) when a qualifying determination has not been made within a defined window
- An affirmative disclosure principle: AI involvement in drafting is disclosable to FDA and the IRB on request as a standing posture, with the Part 11 attribution record producible on inspection — stated in the Constitution so HC7 cannot be read as concealment
- An independence requirement on G4 for Mode B: the qualified safety physician making causality/expectedness determinations must not be the sponsor-investigator whose trial it is, or the conflict must be disclosed to the IRB with the mitigation named
- A no-release-with-unresolved-inferred-flags rule closing the HC2/HC7 seam
