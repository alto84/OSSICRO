# Persona review — Regulatory-Affairs / Legal Compliance Officer

**Reviewer stance:** outside regulatory-affairs / legal compliance counsel assessing the enrolling physician's, the manufacturer's, and the project's own exposure. Static review of the whole repo (constitution, engine, registries, app server, UI, wiki, QA record) as of 2026-07-11; 336/336 tests pass (`python -m pytest engine/tests/ app/tests/ -q`).

## 1. INVENTORY — what the system actually offers this persona today

**The governing legal architecture (docs + registry):**
- `docs/OSSICRO-CONSTITUTION.md` v1.0 — value ordering (safety/compliance > correctness > speed), seven hard constraints (HC1 no non-delegable act; HC2 no fabrication; HC3 computed statutory clocks; HC4 provenance/verbatim integrity; HC5 Part-11 attribution; HC6 no exculpatory consent language; HC7 no process leakage), the earned-rule test, and the one defended line: reserved acts stay with accountable humans at every capability level.
- The **transfer-of-obligations rule** (21 CFR 312.52): stated correctly and repeatedly — software cannot be a sponsor or a transferee; a purported transfer to a non-entity leaves the obligation with the sponsor. Threaded through HC1's justification, `gates.json` (`ind-1571-signature`), the 3926 PDF signature notice (`pdf_3926.py:_SIGNATURE_NOTICE`), the landing page, and a practitioner-grade TORO wiki page that (post-QA) now distinguishes FDA exposure from civil/tort exposure and warns on assumption-in-fact.
- The 19-row non-delegable gating matrix (`wiki/05-ossicro-system/non-delegable-functions-and-gates.md` G1–G19), 8 rows code-enforced in `engine/registry/gates.json` + `engine/ossicro/gates.py` — fail-closed `GateViolation`, role-vs-gate matching, named human + attestation statement required, sign-off validity re-checked at read time (`signoff_problems`), unknown gate fails closed.

**The compliance mechanics (engine + app), verified in code:**
- Drafts-only: every deliverable path is DRAFT-marked (PDF watermark baked into every page's content stream with no removal flag, `pdf_3926.py`; FDF opens with DRAFT lines; UI draftbar; patient view standing notice). `finalize()` is the only path to `final` and requires a valid recorded human sign-off.
- Statutory clocks computed, never judged (HC3): `clocks.py` working-day engine (5 U.S.C. 6103 holidays + OPM observance), 15-working-day 312.310(d)(2), 5-working-day 56.104(c), 30-calendar-day 312.40(b)(1); triggers are physician-entered dates; absent trigger = honestly UNARMED, unparseable = loud `TriggerDateError`; nothing anchors on the wall clock.
- Sponsor-investigator obligations at enrollment (INV-5 `/promote`): named actor + recorded HIPAA legal basis (164.508 / 164.512(i)(1)(i) / 164.506(c)) required; arms an obligations checklist (312.32(c)(1) 15-day, 312.32(c)(2) 7-day, 312.310(d)(2) 15-working-day, 56.104(c) 5-working-day, 312.33 annual via 312.40(b)(1) effective date).
- HIPAA posture: preparatory-review vs enrollment state machine; FHIR intake structurally refuses name/MRN/address/telecom/birthDate values (`fhir_ingest.py`, `never_extracted`); the INV-4 egress gateway (`egress.py`) is a closed frozen struct (codes/band/sex/route only), query decomposition + ICD-10 roll-up against rare-disease inference egress, live egress disabled behind an explicit human-greenlight flag, every query audit-logged; honest residual-risk statement in the module docstring.
- Part-11 substrate (HC5): append-only hash-chained audit trail (`audit.py`, `verify_chain`), value-free detail shape enforced (INV-8), committed-profile input hashes (INV-3), per-span `ProvenanceRecord`s, sign-off records with input hash at signing time and a §6.1 staleness advisory.
- Persona minimization: manufacturer sees released snapshots only (opaque release_id, never case_id); patient view reachable only by opaque token, plain-language, stage-conditional truthful notices; CRO board read-only; the supply decision / LOA signature / FDA authorization / IRB concurrence are modeled only as recorded external facts.
- Disclaimers: MIT LICENSE + appended non-delegable-function notice ("NOT MEDICAL, LEGAL, OR REGULATORY ADVICE"); README hard-line section; app/README "not medical, legal, or regulatory advice"; honesty notes that the prototype is unvalidated and citations need independent verification.

**The QA record** (`qa/`, `docs/ehr-integration/`): six-persona adversarial review with a punch list, a revise log that verified citations against primary sources (including the Form 1571 item-15 correction), and wave-review memos. This is unusually good discovery posture — the project audits itself and writes it down.

## 2. ASSESSMENT — what serves this persona, and where it is weak

**What genuinely serves the compliance persona.** The 312.52 "OSSICRO is never a transferee" line is legally correct, consistently held, and enforced where it matters (no signature, no submission, no consent, no causality — all fail closed in code, not in prose). The fail-closed gate design, the computed-clock discipline with honest UNARMED states, the append-only hash-chained audit trail, the closed egress struct with an explicit residual-risk statement, and the release/token minimization pattern are all better than what most commercial eRegulatory tools document. The Confirmed/Interpretive labeling convention (CP4) is exactly what counsel wants. The drafts-only line and the nothing-auto-decided line are, in the code I read, real: I found no path that finalizes, sends, signs, decides, or files, and no finding layer that can clear a gate (escalate-only verified in `pipeline.py`).

**Where the exposure is under-addressed or overclaimed.**
1. **The live concept reviewer is a designed-in PHI egress that the rest of the system's own rules would forbid** (Finding L1, BLOCKER). The egress gateway demands an explicit human greenlight to send *rolled-up condition codes*; the concept reviewer sends *entire rendered documents* — diagnosis, prior therapies, clinical rationale, physician identity, coded patient id — to Anthropic's API automatically whenever an environment variable is set, with no BAA anywhere in the repo, no de-identification, no named-human act, no audit record, and a UI that promises "never leaves this machine."
2. **Two deadline-correctness defects inside the system's flagship guarantee (HC3)**: the 56.104(c) clock armed from the wrong anchor in the obligations checklist, and the 312.310(c)(2) end-of-treatment summary drafted into the treatment plan but absent from the tracker. HC3 is the promise a physician-sponsor is buying; these are the misses that create the physician's exposure.
3. **Part 11 aspiration written in the present tense**: the wiki's clause-by-clause table claims access control and e-signature machinery the prototype does not have (the code itself is honest; the wiki is not labeled BUILT/NOT-YET-BUILT), and HC5's "every AI contribution is logged... with the disposition of the human reviewer" is not implemented for the concept layer.
4. **"No real PHI" is a pilot assumption the intake design quietly undermines** (initials invited, unencrypted unauthenticated local store, manufacturer/CRO views exposing diagnosis + physician).
5. **The MIT license cannot carry the weight the constitution puts on "no configuration flag or API path goes around it"** — true of this repo, not of a fork; the notice is a notice, not an enforceable use restriction.
6. **The disclaimer never reaches the working physician**: "not medical, legal, or regulatory advice" lives in LICENSE and READMEs; the app UI where the physician actually acts never says it.

**Hard-line check (drafts-only / no-PHI-egress / nothing-auto-decided):** drafts-only and nothing-auto-decided hold everywhere I looked. No-PHI-egress is violated **in design (latent)** by the live-reviewer path — flagged as the BLOCKER below. It does not fire in the shipped default (no key set, stub reviewer answers), but it fires with zero further code change the moment the documented "production" configuration is used.

### Larger issues

**L1. [LARGER-ISSUE — BLOCKER] The live Claude concept reviewer is an ungated PHI egress path that contradicts the system's own no-PHI-egress design, its UI promises, and its HIPAA lawful-basis argument.**
Where: `app/server.py:562–576` (`_select_reviewer`) — `if os.environ.get("ANTHROPIC_API_KEY"): ... return ClaudeConceptReviewer.from_anthropic()` — invoked on **every** `GET /check` (`_check_payload`, line 790) and `GET /package` (`_package_payload`, line 898); `engine/ossicro/review_claude.py:96–105` sends the full rendered document text (`_build_user_message` → `messages.create`) to Anthropic's API.
What leaves the machine when the key is set: the rendered cover letter, 3926, treatment plan, and ICF drafts — i.e., `patient.coded_id` (which the intake help text says may be **initials**), age, sex, free-text diagnosis, prior therapies, clinical rationale, and the physician's full identity. For an n-of-1 rare-disease case this is realistically identifiable PHI (the project's own QA said exactly this about "de-identified" n-of-1 data: `qa/PUNCH-LIST.md` HIGH, hipaa-and-privacy-gating).
Why this is a hard-line violation in design:
- The UI promises the opposite: `app/static/index.html:585` — "Your chart data is parsed locally and **never leaves this machine**".
- The wiki's lawful-basis argument for preparatory review is conditional on exactly this not happening: `wiki/05-ossicro-system/hipaa-and-privacy-gating.md:33` — "lawful **provided PHI does not leave the covered entity**".
- The system's own egress gateway (`engine/ossicro/egress.py:87–91`) requires an explicit human greenlight flag ("must not be flipped by code, config, or a test") to send *de-identified condition codes*; the reviewer path sends *whole documents* on the strength of an environment variable, silently, with **no audit record of the outbound call** (contrast: every egress_query is audited), no BAA anywhere in the repo (`grep -ri "business associate"` returns nothing), and no de-identification pass.
- `egress.py`'s module docstring designates `review_claude.py` as the only other module "permitted to import an outbound HTTP client" — the boundary test knows this path exists, but nothing subjects it to the INV-4 discipline.
Exposure: for the physician (covered-entity workforce member), an impermissible disclosure the moment a real chart value is in the case; for the project, the flagship privacy claim is falsifiable by reading two files.
Direction: (a) put the live reviewer behind the same explicit-greenlight pattern as `_LIVE_EGRESS_ENABLED` — a named-human act recorded in the audit trail, never an env-var default; (b) write an `ai_review` audit record (model, doc ids, input hash, destination) for every live call, which HC5 requires anyway (see L5); (c) document the BAA/zero-retention requirement as a precondition in the constitution or the deployment doc; (d) reconcile `index.html:585` so the promise is scoped to chart *import* and states plainly when and where document text goes for AI review; (e) consider reviewing only documents rendered from a synthetic/de-identified projection until (a)–(c) exist.

**L2. [LARGER-ISSUE — MAJOR] The 56.104(c) 5-working-day IRB clock in the post-enrollment obligations checklist is computed from the wrong anchor date, contradicting the regulation, the project's own spec, and its own /check screen.**
Where: `app/server.py:1249–1274` (`_enrollment_obligations`) arms **both** emergency clocks from `submission.emergency_auth_datetime` via `clocks.expanded_access_emergency_deadlines(auth_date)`; `engine/ossicro/clocks.py:137–148` computes both deadlines from the single `authorization_date` (docstring: "The two clocks that start when FDA authorizes emergency use by phone").
The regulation and the project's own artifacts anchor the IRB clock on the **treatment**, not the phone authorization: 21 CFR 56.104(c) (report of the emergency *use*); `engine/registry/routes.json:134` (`"trigger_field": "submission.first_treatment_date"`); `docs/route-3926-submission-spec.md` §3.2 step 5 and §3.3 clock table ("Trigger: First treatment administered"); the intake field `submission.first_treatment_date` exists precisely for this (routes.json:45). Even the server's own strings contradict each other in one payload: `obligation_irb_notify` correctly says "within 5 working days **of the treatment**" while `obligation_irb_notify_q` (server.py:337–340) instructs "Enter the **FDA telephone-authorization date** … to arm the 5-working-day IRB-notification deadline."
Consequence: the /check screen and the /promote checklist can display **two different due dates for the same statutory duty**; when treatment lags authorization the checklist date is early (annoying), and any future code path that reuses the clocks helper with the treatment date absent will silently substitute the wrong trigger. HC3's entire justification is that a computed clock is strictly better than a judged one — a computed clock with the wrong anchor is worse than either, because it is confidently wrong.
Direction: in `_enrollment_obligations`, arm the 312.310(d)(2) clock from `emergency_auth_datetime` and the 56.104(c) clock from `first_treatment_date` (each honestly UNARMED when its own trigger is absent); fix `obligation_irb_notify_q`; split or re-document `expanded_access_emergency_deadlines` so the two clocks take their own anchors; add a reconciliation test mirroring `ClockReconciliationTests`.

**L3. [LARGER-ISSUE — MAJOR] The obligations checklist omits the 312.310(c)(2) end-of-treatment written summary — an obligation OSSICRO's own generated document promises FDA.**
Where: the generated treatment plan commits the physician: `engine/ossicro/ea_generators.py:617–621` — "…provide the summary/annual report per 21 CFR 312.310(c)(2)." But `_enrollment_obligations` (`app/server.py:1214–1294`) tracks only 312.32(c)(1)/(c)(2), 312.310(d)(2), 56.104(c), and 312.33. Nothing anywhere tracks the conclusion-of-treatment written summary.
Why it matters: the system drafts a commitment and then drops it from the operative tracker. The checklist's own design note says "the system must not walk a physician into duties it then ignores" (server.py:1180–1181) — this is exactly such a duty, and for a single-patient EA it is the one obligation that is *guaranteed* to come due. A physician who relies on the checklist as the complete duty list (the UI calls each row "a duty the enrollment creates") will believe the list is exhaustive.
Direction: add an always-present, unarmed checklist row ("Written summary of the expanded access use, including adverse effects, at the conclusion of treatment — 21 CFR 312.310(c)(2); trigger: treatment-conclusion date, to be recorded"), plus intake support for a treatment-conclusion date. While there, state on the checklist that it is the *tracked subset*, not the entirety, of sponsor-investigator duties (312.50, 312.57, 312.62 recordkeeping etc. are untracked).

**L4. [LARGER-ISSUE — MAJOR] The obligations checklist is empty until the app-internal promote() event, but the legal duties attach by operation of law — a physician who treats without clicking "Record enrollment" sees no obligations anywhere.**
Where: `app/server.py:1238–1239` — `if not isinstance(case.get("enrollment"), dict): return []` — with the design comment "Empty until enrollment — the duties attach at the INV-5 transition, not before." UI reinforces it: `app/static/index.html:1108–1110` — enrollment "records the legal basis and the ongoing sponsor-investigator duties **it creates**"; checklist note: "Each row is a duty **the enrollment creates**."
Why it matters: 312.32/312.33/312.310 duties attach to the physician-sponsor when the IND is in effect and treatment proceeds — facts, not app events. INV-5 is a *HIPAA-basis* transition; it is legally correct as that, but the code has made it the switch for the *FDA-obligations* display too. The realistic failure: an emergency-use physician records the authorization date, treats, never uses the promote screen (nothing forces them to), and the 5-working-day IRB notice and 15-working-day written 3926 never appear in the obligations view. (They do appear in /check clocks on the emergency route — partial mitigation — but the annual report and safety-report duties appear nowhere without promote.)
Direction: decouple. Surface the obligations checklist (armed or honestly unarmed) as soon as the facts that create the duties are recorded — emergency authorization date entered, or FDA receipt date entered — independent of the INV-5 promote; keep promote as the HIPAA-basis record it actually is; reword "duties it creates" → "duties this treatment use creates; OSSICRO shows them from the moment the anchoring facts are recorded."

**L5. [LARGER-ISSUE — MAJOR] Part-11 overclaim: the wiki states target controls in the present tense that the prototype does not have, and HC5's attribution promise is not implemented for the concept-review layer.**
Where:
- `wiki/05-ossicro-system/part-11-and-ai-credibility.md` clause table: "11.10(d) … **Role-scoped access mirroring the four personas**" — but persona authentication is explicitly deferred (INV-7; `STRINGS["cro_board_note"]`: "Persona-level authentication is deferred") and every endpoint is unauthenticated. Same page: "**Every gate signature** in the master gating matrix **runs on this machinery**" (§11.100–11.300 e-signatures) — but `engine/ossicro/gates.py:74–77` honestly says "In production this call sits behind an authenticated 21 CFR Part 11 e-signature ceremony; **the prototype enforces attribution and role only**." No identity verification, no §11.100(c) certification, nothing binding a signature to a person exists.
- HC5 (`docs/OSSICRO-CONSTITUTION.md:47`): "Every AI contribution is logged with the model, its version, the input hash, a timestamp, and the **identity and disposition of the human reviewer**." In the app, `_check_payload` runs the concept reviewer and returns findings to the browser but writes **no audit record** of the AI contribution (no `audit_mod.append` on that path), and no mechanism captures the human's disposition of any concept finding at all. The ReviewReport's model/timestamp die with the HTTP response.
Why it matters: this is the exact overclaim pattern the project's CP4 forbids in generated documents, committed in its own compliance documentation. A BIMO inspector or a pharma partner's QA auditor reading the wiki against the code will conclude the compliance narrative is aspirational — which then taints the claims that are real (the audit chain, the gates).
Direction: (a) add a BUILT / NOT-YET-BUILT (or Confirmed/Interpretive/Planned) status column to the Part-11 clause table, consistent with the labeling discipline used everywhere else; (b) write one audit record per concept-review invocation (action `ai_review`: model, doc ids, consumed input hash) — cheap, and it makes HC5 true for the judgment layer; (c) either build a finding-disposition capture (accept/reject per concept finding, named human) or narrow HC5's text to what is enforced today.

**L6. [LARGER-ISSUE — MAJOR] "No real PHI" is a pilot assumption the product design undermines: initials invited into an unencrypted, unauthenticated store, with no covered-entity deployment guidance and no BAA inventory.**
Where: `engine/registry/routes.json:4` — coded-id help text: "A code **or initials** only." Cases persist as cleartext JSON in `app/data/cases/` (server.py:7, "no real PHI; coded identifiers only"); `GET /api/manufacturer/inbox` and `GET /api/cro/board` expose diagnosis + physician + coded id with no authentication (INV-7 deferred, honestly noted, and the server binds 127.0.0.1 — both mitigations are one deployment decision away from gone).
Why it matters: initials + age band + rare diagnosis + the treating physician's full identity is realistically identifiable (the QA record itself says de-identification fails at n=1). Form FDA 3926 legitimately asks for initials on the *submission*; OSSICRO's *store* is not the submission, and nothing tells a deployer that running this on a shared machine, or off-localhost, or with real cases, changes its HIPAA character completely. There is no BAA discussion anywhere in the repo, no encryption-at-rest note, no retention/deletion story for case JSONs, and no "what changes when the first real patient enters" document.
Direction: a short deployment-compliance page (covered-entity boundary; the store is PHI the moment a real case exists; encryption-at-rest expectation; BAA inventory covering any model API and any host; retention/deletion) referenced from README and app/README; change the coded-id help text to prefer a study-assigned code over initials; make INV-7 a precondition, not a deferral, for any non-synthetic pilot.

**L7. [LARGER-ISSUE — MINOR] The MIT license cannot carry the constitution's "no configuration flag or API path goes around it" claim, and the appended notice is not an enforceable restriction.**
Where: `LICENSE` (MIT + "NOTICE — … must not be configured to perform …"); `docs/OSSICRO-CONSTITUTION.md:39` ("no configuration flag or API path goes around it").
Why it matters: both statements are true of *this repository's code* and silent about the ecosystem MIT creates — any fork may delete `gates.py`, flip `_LIVE_EGRESS_ENABLED`, or strip the DRAFT watermark, then operate under the OSSICRO name. The notice reads like a use restriction but grants/withholds nothing (MIT already granted modification); a court reads it as a disclaimer at best. The project's liability framing ("the gates fail closed") is thus accurate for the artifact and overbroad as a claim about "OSSICRO" the product-in-the-world.
Direction: one honest paragraph in README/constitution scoping the hard line to this codebase; consider trademark protection for the OSSICRO name so gate-stripped forks cannot trade on it; optionally a conformance statement ("this build enforces HC1–HC7") that downstream distributors must remove if they alter the gate layer.

**L8. [LARGER-ISSUE — MINOR] HC6's justification overstates what a 4-regex tripwire can close.**
Where: `docs/OSSICRO-CONSTITUTION.md:49` — "the regulation prohibits a **discrete, identifiable class** of constructions, so a deterministic check is the correct tool, and it stays correct for as long as the regulation stands" — vs `engine/registry/banned-constructions.json:13–16`: four patterns (`you waive`, `release … from liability`, `shall/will not be liable`, `give up … rights`).
Why it matters: exculpatory language is an open class (FDA/OHRP's own guidance examples include "hold harmless," "you will bear the costs regardless of fault," no-fault waivers phrased affirmatively). "I can't sue" phrasings, hold-harmless constructions, and cost-shifting-as-waiver all sail past the current regexes; the concept reviewer is the real backstop but HC6's text claims closure for the deterministic tier. For counsel, the risk is a consent draft that passes the "hard" HC6 check and is read as HC6-clean.
Direction: reword HC6's justification to "the deterministic tier hard-fails the enumerated constructions; judgment owns the class" (mirroring the tier-A/tier-B language already in `banned-constructions.json`), and grow the tier-A list from the FDA exculpatory-language draft guidance examples.

**L9. [LARGER-ISSUE — MINOR] The 312.52 truth is everywhere except the point of use, and re-signing silently replaces the prior operative sign-off record.**
(a) The physician-facing app never states, where the physician acts, that using OSSICRO transfers nothing — "you remain the sponsor-investigator; OSSICRO is not and can never be a 312.52 transferee; no obligation moves to this software or its operators." The landing page and wiki carry it; the commit/promote/sign-off screens (where reliance forms) do not. One sentence on the sign-off modal and the promote card would put the transfer-of-obligations rule where it protects the physician (and the project) most.
(b) `app/server.py:1447–1450` (`_record_signoff`): a re-recorded sign-off for the same (gate_id, doc_id) *replaces* the prior record in `case["signoffs"]` (the append-only audit trail retains the act, but not the superseded record's full content/statement). Under 11.10(e)'s "changes must not obscure previously recorded information," the operative record set should supersede, not overwrite — append with a `superseded_by`/`superseded_at` marker instead of filtering the old record out.
