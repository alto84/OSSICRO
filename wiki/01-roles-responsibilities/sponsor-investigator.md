# Sponsor-Investigator — The Dual Role under 21 CFR 312.3(b)

> [!authority] Governing authority
> 21 CFR 312.3(b) (definition); by incorporation, the full sponsor set (312.50-312.59, plus 312.20-312.23, 312.30-312.33) and the full investigator set (312.60-312.69); FDA, *Overview of Sponsor-Investigator Roles and Responsibilities*; FDA Draft Guidance, *Investigational New Drug Applications Prepared and Submitted by Sponsor-Investigators* (May 2015 — **draft, not finalized; represents FDA's current thinking only**). Status: **Mixed** — the dual-obligation rule is confirmed; the OSSICRO tractability thesis is interpretive and marked.

A **sponsor-investigator** is "an individual who both initiates and conducts an investigation, and under whose immediate direction the investigational drug is administered or dispensed." The term **"does not include any person other than an individual"** — a corporation, institution, or software system cannot be a sponsor-investigator. "The requirements applicable to a sponsor-investigator under this part include both those applicable to an investigator and a sponsor" (21 CFR 312.3(b)). This is the operative structure of every investigator-initiated trial (IIT/IIS) and the core OSSICRO user: one physician lawfully carries what a conventional trial splits between a pharma company and a site. The role is legally routine and operationally crushing — Harvard Catalyst's consult-service literature states that investigator-sponsors "often do not fully appreciate their regulatory obligations nor have resources to ensure compliance" (Kim et al. 2014, PMID [24455986](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24455986/)). OSSICRO exists to make this role tractable ([[what-is-ossicro]], [[guiding-scenario]]).

## What the collapse means legally

Three consequences follow directly from 312.3(b):

1. **Both obligation sets apply in full.** Nothing about holding both roles reduces either set. The sponsor-investigator owes every duty on the [[sponsor]] page and every duty on the [[investigator]] page, simultaneously.
2. **No self-delegation.** The sponsor duties cannot be delegated away to oneself, to staff, or to software. Specific *tasks* may be delegated to qualified staff (with a [[delegation-of-authority-log]]), and enumerated *sponsor obligations* may be transferred in writing to a legally accountable CRO under 21 CFR 312.52 ([[transfer-of-regulatory-obligations-toro]], [[micro-cro-accountable-layer]]) — but investigator conduct obligations are not transferable at all, and any obligation not covered by a 312.52 writing remains with the sponsor-investigator.
3. **Only an individual.** Because the definition excludes non-individuals, the accountable center of an investigator-initiated IND is always a named human being. This is the affirmative half of the OSSICRO legal frame ([[legal-thesis-3123-vs-31252]]): the law itself provides a lawful single-human structure, so no disintermediation of an accountable entity is needed or attempted.

## The combined obligation set

### As sponsor (21 CFR 312 Subparts B, C, D)

- **Prepare, submit, and hold the IND** (312.20, 312.22, 312.23): the full content-and-format package — [[form-fda-1571-ind-cover|Form FDA 1571]] cover, general investigational plan, [[investigators-brochure|investigator's brochure]] (where required), protocol, CMC, pharmacology/toxicology, prior human experience ([[ind-application-312-23]], [[pre-ind-and-ind-preparation]]). Observe the **30-day safe-to-proceed period** before dosing, and respond to any clinical hold ([[ind-submission-and-30-day-clock]]).
- **Maintain an effective IND** (312.50): protocol amendments (312.30), information amendments (312.31), and prompt safety communication.
- **IND safety reporting** (312.32): the 15-calendar-day IND safety report for serious and unexpected suspected adverse reactions and qualifying findings, and the 7-calendar-day notification for unexpected fatal or life-threatening suspected adverse reactions ([[ind-safety-report]], [[form-fda-3500a-medwatch]], [[safety-reporting-lifecycle]]). A plain investigator reports SAEs only to the sponsor (312.64(b)); the sponsor-investigator faces FDA directly.
- **Annual report** (312.33): due within 60 days of the IND anniversary date ([[ind-annual-report-dsur]], [[annual-reporting-and-amendments]]).
- **The 312.53 package — executed on oneself**: the sponsor-investigator obtains and maintains their own signed [[form-fda-1572-statement-of-investigator|Form FDA 1572]], current CV, protocol, and Part 54 financial-interest information ([[form-fda-3454-3455-financial-disclosure]]) — self-certification/disclosure of one's own covered arrangements is required, and the 1572 is retained in the IND file, not submitted separately by a site.
- **Ensure proper monitoring** (312.50, 312.53(d), 312.56(a)): even a single-site, single-investigator study requires a monitoring approach proportionate to risk (ICH E6(R3); [[monitoring-plan]], [[risk-based-monitoring-e6r3]]); a qualified independent monitor is the accepted practice when the S-I cannot credibly monitor their own conduct.
- **Sponsor records, inspection, and drug disposition** (312.57, 312.58, 312.59): shipment/disposition records, financial-interest records, 2-year retention, FDA (and DEA where applicable) access, and return or authorized disposition of unused drug.
- **The safety-risk decisions** (312.56(b), (d)): securing compliance, and discontinuing an investigation presenting unreasonable and significant risk within 5 working days, with notification of FDA, IRBs, and investigators.

### As investigator (21 CFR 312.60-312.69)

Protocol adherence and subject protection (312.60); personal supervision and drug control (312.61); disposition records, case histories, and 2-year retention (312.62); progress, safety, final, and financial reports (312.64); IRB assurance and the no-unapproved-change rule (312.66); FDA inspection of records (312.68); controlled-substance security (312.69); informed consent per Part 50. See [[investigator]] for the full enumeration.

> [!warning] Non-delegable
> The sponsor-investigator personally owns, and OSSICRO only drafts toward: (1) the informed-consent event with each subject ([[informed-consent-document-vs-event]]); (2) assurance of IRB review and the enrollment gate on documented approval ([[irb-iec]]); (3) SAE seriousness/causality/expectedness judgments and the decision to file 7-/15-day reports (312.32, 312.64(b)); (4) the Form FDA 1571 and 1572 signatures and Part 54 attestations; (5) every submission to FDA; (6) supervision of the investigation and the 312.56 compliance/discontinuation decisions. These do not move — not to staff, not to a CRO except as 312.52 permits for sponsor obligations, and never to software.

## What FDA has said about the role

FDA's *Overview of Sponsor-Investigator Roles and Responsibilities* consolidates the dual duty set for individual IND holders, and the May 2015 draft guidance *Investigational New Drug Applications Prepared and Submitted by Sponsor-Investigators* walks through what an S-I IND must contain — including the use of existing literature or a right-of-reference to a manufacturer's IND in lieu of original CMC and toxicology data, and substitution of the approved package insert for an investigator's brochure when studying a marketed drug (cf. 312.55, 312.23(a)(5)). The 2015 guidance remains a **draft**; OSSICRO cites it as FDA's current thinking, not binding law. For studies of lawfully marketed drugs, the S-I should first check the IND-exemption criteria of 21 CFR 312.2(b) before assuming an IND is required.

## The OSSICRO thesis for this role

> [!interpretive] OSSICRO position
> The sponsor-investigator role is impractical for a solo clinician not because the judgments are too many, but because the *coordination labor* is: assembling the 312.23 package, tracking the 30-day and 7-/15-day and 60-day clocks, maintaining the TMF against ICH E6(R3) Appendix C, versioning amendments, and keeping every record inspection-ready. That labor is exactly where conventional CRO cost concentrates, and it is automatable. OSSICRO's design: the [[generate-check-validate-engine]] drafts and completeness-checks every artifact ([[completeness-ledger]]); the [[safety-clock-engine]] computes every deadline; and every accountable act above the automation line routes to the named human at a gate ([[non-delegable-functions-and-gates]]). Where the S-I genuinely cannot hold a sponsor obligation personally, the transfer target is the legally accountable [[micro-cro-accountable-layer]] via a written [[transfer-of-regulatory-obligations-toro|TORO]] — never the software. OSSICRO drafts COMPLETE documentation for qualified human review; it never replaces the sponsor-investigator's judgment, IRB/ethics review, DSMB oversight ([[dsmb-dmc]]), or any medical or safety decision.

## Boundary cases

- **Mode A instead:** if a pharma company sponsors the protocol, the physician is a site [[investigator]] only — a far lighter burden ([[two-modes-site-vs-sponsor-investigator]], [[pharma-partner-sponsor]]).
- **Single-patient treatment:** where the goal is treatment access rather than research, the expanded-access pathway (21 CFR 312 Subpart I; [[form-fda-3926-expanded-access]]) may fit better than a research IND ([[expanded-access-workflow]], [[single-patient-site-enrollment]]).
- **Pharma-supported IIS:** a manufacturer may supply drug and funding for an S-I study without becoming sponsor; the S-I remains the IND holder ([[iis-request-workflow]]).
