# IND Safety Report Timelines — The 7-Day and 15-Day Clocks

> [!authority] Governing authority
> 21 CFR 312.32 sets two expedited **IND safety report** clocks: a **15-calendar-day** report for a serious-and-unexpected suspected adverse reaction ([312.32(c)(1)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32)) and a **7-calendar-day** report for an unexpected fatal or life-threatening one ([312.32(c)(2)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32)). The definitions live at [312.32(a)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32); the current framework was established by [Federal Register 75 FR 59935 (Sept 29, 2010)](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/FR-2010-09-29/html/2010-24296.htm) and harmonizes with ICH E2A. Status: **Confirmed** — black-letter reporting requirements. OSSICRO's computation-and-escalation role is interpretive and marked inline.

Two clocks govern expedited IND safety reporting, and both start only after a **human determines** that a report is owed. The clocks are **calendar days**, not business days. This page defines the trigger terms and the two timelines and their follow-up obligations; the end-to-end routing to FDA, investigators, the IRB, and the DSMB is on [[safety-reporting-workflow]], and the reporting format is on [[form-fda-3500a-medwatch]].

## The four defining terms (21 CFR 312.32(a))

Whether a report is owed — and which clock applies — turns on four definitions. All four are the sponsor's medical judgment, not a mechanical lookup.

- **Serious adverse event / suspected adverse reaction.** An event that results in any of: **death**, a **life-threatening** situation, **inpatient hospitalization or prolongation** of hospitalization, a **persistent or significant disability/incapacity**, a **congenital anomaly/birth defect**, or a **medically important** event that may jeopardize the patient and may require intervention to prevent one of the above outcomes.
- **Life-threatening.** The patient was, in the view of the investigator or sponsor, **at substantial risk of death at the time of the event** — not that the event hypothetically could have been fatal had it progressed.
- **Unexpected.** An adverse event **not listed** in the [[investigators-brochure]] (or, for a marketed drug, the general investigational plan / labeling), or **listed but of greater severity or specificity** than described. Expectedness is defined against the *current* IB, so an [[investigators-brochure|IB update]] recalibrates it for every subsequent case.
- **Suspected adverse reaction.** An adverse event for which there is a **reasonable possibility** that the drug caused it — "reasonable possibility" meaning there is evidence to suggest a causal relationship. This is a **lower threshold than "reasonably related"** but **higher than mere temporal association**; it is the causality determination at the heart of the gate.

A case that is **serious AND unexpected AND a suspected adverse reaction** is a SUSAR — the trigger for expedited reporting.

## The 15-day clock — 21 CFR 312.32(c)(1)

The sponsor must notify FDA and **all participating investigators** in an IND safety report **as soon as possible but no later than 15 calendar days** after the sponsor determines the information qualifies, for:

- Any **suspected adverse reaction that is both serious and unexpected** (an individual SUSAR).
- **Findings from other sources** that suggest a significant risk to humans — clinical or epidemiologic studies, pooled/aggregate analyses showing the drug caused an event, or findings from animal or in vitro testing (e.g., carcinogenicity, teratogenicity).
- A **clinically important increased rate of occurrence** of a serious suspected adverse reaction over the rate listed in the protocol or IB.

The clock starts on the sponsor's **determination** that the case qualifies — the day the safety physician's causality/seriousness/expectedness assessment resolves to "reportable" — not necessarily the day of first receipt.

## The 7-day clock — 21 CFR 312.32(c)(2)

Any **unexpected fatal or life-threatening suspected adverse reaction** must be reported to FDA **as soon as possible but no later than 7 calendar days** after the sponsor's **initial receipt of the information** (by any means — telephone, facsimile, or electronic). The 7-day report may be an initial notification; it must be followed by a **complete written report** submitted as a **15-day IND safety report** ([312.32(c)(2)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32)).

Note the two clocks start on different events: the **7-day clock runs from initial receipt** of the information (fatal/life-threatening cases are urgent enough that the clock does not wait for full assessment), while the **15-day clock runs from the qualifying determination**. OSSICRO must model both start events correctly.

| | 7-day report | 15-day report |
|---|---|---|
| **Trigger** | Unexpected fatal **or** life-threatening suspected adverse reaction | Serious + unexpected suspected adverse reaction; qualifying aggregate/other findings; clinically important rate increase |
| **Deadline** | ≤ 7 calendar days | ≤ 15 calendar days |
| **Clock starts** | Sponsor's **initial receipt** of the information | Sponsor's **determination** that it qualifies |
| **Follow-up** | Complete written report as a 15-day IND safety report | Follow-up reports with new information ([312.32(d)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32)) |
| **Recipients** | FDA + all participating investigators | FDA + all participating investigators |

## Ongoing review and follow-up obligations

- **Ongoing safety review — [312.32(b)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32).** The sponsor must promptly review **all** safety information from any source, foreign or domestic, on a continuing basis — the surveillance duty that feeds the clocks.
- **Follow-up — [312.32(d)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32).** The sponsor must promptly investigate and submit **follow-up reports** with additional information as it becomes available; if additional information turns a previously non-qualifying event into a reportable one, the applicable clock runs from that new determination.
- **Aggregate rollup — [312.33](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.33).** All reports roll up into the annual [[ind-annual-report-dsur|IND annual report / DSUR]].

## The gate — the clock is computable; the trigger is not

> [!warning] Non-delegable
> **What starts each clock is a medical judgment**: the seriousness, life-threatening, expectedness, and causality determinations under [21 CFR 312.32(a)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32) are owned by the sponsor's qualified [[medical-monitor|medical monitor / safety physician]] (in the [[sponsor-investigator]] model, the physician wearing the sponsor hat). OSSICRO must never auto-classify a case as a SUSAR, never machine-decide "life-threatening," never machine-set expectedness against the IB, and never machine-adjudicate "reasonable possibility of a causal relationship." The engine computes the deadline **once a human sets the trigger** and escalates as time runs; it does not manufacture the trigger, and **submission to FDA is a human act**.

> [!interpretive] OSSICRO position
> The **timeliness layer is computable; the classification layer is not.** The [[safety-clock-engine]] takes the human's determination and the correct start-event (initial receipt for the 7-day; qualifying determination for the 15-day), computes the calendar-day deadline, and escalates through the [[communication-hub]] as the clock approaches — but it computes and escalates, it does not file and does not make the causality call. A drafted report carries an explicit "awaiting causality/seriousness/expectedness determination by [named medical monitor]" gate on the [[completeness-ledger]] until the human closes it. Modeling **two distinct clock-start events** correctly (receipt vs. determination) is a deliberate design requirement, because conflating them under-counts the 7-day urgency.

## OSSICRO engine behavior

- **Generate:** on a captured serious event, drafts the report shell and pre-computes both candidate deadlines (7-day from receipt; 15-day from an anticipated determination date) pending the human's classification.
- **Check:** confirms the case carries an identifiable patient, reporter, suspect product, and event, and the IND/protocol numbers, before the report can be authorized.
- **Validate:** holds the report behind the [312.32(a)](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-312/subpart-B/section-312.32) determination gate; once the safety physician classifies the case, resolves the applicable clock and start-event, escalates as the deadline runs, and opens the human-authorized FDA-submission and all-investigator notification tasks — never advancing past the gate on its own.
